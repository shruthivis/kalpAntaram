Such information to be gathered from the Vedic Literature is not quite as meagre as is commonly supposed. The foregoing brief discussion will make it clear that the later rules contained in the dharms≈´tras and other works on dharma≈õƒÅstra had their roots deep down in the most ancient Vedic tradition and that the authors of the dharmas≈´tras were quite justified in looking up to the Vedas as a source of dharma. Before proceeding further a few preliminary remarks must be made. Ancient Sanskrit writers and modern historians of Sanskrit Literature divide ancient Sanskrit works into three groups, viz. the Vedic SamhitƒÅs (·πögveda, Yajurveda, SƒÅmaveda and Atharvaveda ), the BrƒÅhma·πáas (including the early Upani·π£ads like the B·πõhadƒÅra·πáyaka) and S≈´tras. S≈´tras are not Veda, but many of them are connected with the Veda and contain numerous mantras. Kalpa is one of the six auxiliary lores (a·πÖgas) of the Veda and this group is generally later in time than the BrƒÅhma·πáas, though some s≈´tra works appear to have been composed even in the times of the Tai. ƒÄr. II. 10 and of the B·πõhadƒÅra·πáyakopani·π£ad (II.4.10 and IV.5.11). A distinction is drawn between Kalpa and Kalpas≈´tra by the TantravƒÅrtika.30 The word Kalpa (or rather Kalpas≈´tra) is used in two senses, one comprehensive including the aphoristic works on Vedic ritual, on the domestic ceremonies and also on law, government and administration of justice; the other sense covers only those aphoristic works that deal with Vedic sacrifices and matters related thereto. If the first sense is taken then Kalpas≈´tras are classified into three classes, viz.(1) ≈örautas≈´tras that deal with solemn Vedic sacrifices, mentioned or discussed in the Vedas and BrƒÅhma·πáas; (2) G·πõhyas≈´tras that deal with domestic ceremonies such as Upanayana, marriage and with daily and periodical rites and employ mantras for them mostly from one ≈öƒÅkhƒÅ of the Veda; (3) Dharmas≈´tras (also depending on the Vedas as the highest authority) that treat of some of the topics dealt with in the G·πõhyas≈´tras but add provisions on matters concerning economic life, politics, government, civil and criminal law. A complete Kalpa in the first sense should cover all the three divisions. It is highly doubtful whether each Veda ≈öƒÅkhƒÅ had originally a complete set of the three kinds of works covered under the word Kalpa. This will be briefly dealt with a little below.

Recently (1959) Dr. Ram Gopal has brought out a large work on ‘India of Vedic Kalpas≈´tras’ containing over five hundred closely printed pages. The title of the work is rather misleading. It does not deal with the ≈örautas≈´tras beyond very briefly stating what they contain and distinguishing their contents from those of the G·πõhya and Dharmas≈´tras. His work is concerned only with the details gathered from the G·πõhya and Dharmas≈´tras and he has nothing to say about the development of the several topics dealt with in these two classes during the long period of at least 2000 years after the s≈´tra period (which he places between 800 - 500 B. As far as it goes it is a tolerable thesis, though rather prolix, dogmatic and over-disputatious. Having confined himself to the G·πõhya and Dharmas≈´tras, it should have been his business to discuss all points concerning at least the principal G·πõhya and Dharmas≈´tras. But he leaves important matters concerning several s≈´tras untouched. For example, on p. 54 he observes ‘many s≈´tras in the Gaut. Dh. One feels that he should himself have tackled that task at least about some representative G·πõhya s≈´tras and the Gautama-dharma-s≈´tra (which he places on p. 84 among the oldest class of ≈örauta, G·πõhya and Dharmas≈´tras and when on p. 82 he holds that Gautama is undoubtedly the oldest writer on SmƒÅrta Dharma) by a thorough examination of chapters and passages in them and should have pointed out the criteria why certain s≈´tras are held to be spurious and so forth. He need not have included in the thesis the description of the flora and fauna &c. (pp. 103-109) or if he wanted to include these subjects he should have devoted more space. Nor was there any need for him to discuss (as he does on pp. 100-103 ) the varying limits of ƒÄryƒÅvarta, since that subject had been dealt with at length by me in H. of Dh. Vol. II pp. 11-16. Dh, pp. 11-16 and added bits of information that did not occur therein.
